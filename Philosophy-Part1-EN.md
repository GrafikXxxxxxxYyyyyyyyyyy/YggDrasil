# Part 1. The Fundamental Ground of the Philosophy

### 1. The One (*Единое*)

At the first stage we are given the ungraspable, flickering in its existence, all-filling essence of the cosmos (*мироздание*). It is given to us as a single medium that encompasses all processes, all meanings, and all matter. It has no outside: there is nothing to set against it as a whole, no empty place from which one could look upon it; everything thinkable and actual is already contained within its integrity. All possible, all thinkable compositions are not introduced into this essence from without, for they have nowhere to “take place” outside it—they are possible only as events within that same medium. It is utterly ungraspable to dissecting or descriptive thought and at the same time absolutely full and one. Everything that will be spoken of henceforth will be not an addition from without but a bringing-to-light, an unfolding of what is already concealed in this fullness. Unlike Hegel, we do not begin with the concepts of pure being (*бытие*) and pure nothing (*ничто*) and their subsequent identification and synthesis. Here a different point of departure is proposed: both being and nothing are already contained in this primordial fullness as inseparable moments of it itself. Thus what is primary is not the distinction and synthesis of concepts but their undivided unity. Logic and description arise later, when we are already moving within the compositions that have been brought to light. To speak of ungraspability and indivisibility is not to describe from the outside; it is to fix the limit of descriptive thought itself, which discovers that its own existence is secondary and unfolds already within the acknowledged fullness. The designations “being,” “nothing,” “medium,” “substance” belong already to the next step, to what will become the language and logic of the compositions of the cosmos. The first transition is thus the recognition of this all-encompassing, ungraspable fullness and the acceptance that every depth of the composition of the cosmos is already enclosed in it and can unfold only from it. Henceforth, for the sake of descriptive simplicity, we shall name this essence simply **the One** (*Единое*).

### 2. The Impulse of Desire (*Импульс желания*)

The all-encompassingly full and absolutely ungraspable One, flickering in its existence, given to us in the first transition and accepted as the beginning, leaves open the question of whence this “flickering” proceeds—in other words, whence this dynamism comes. We are compelled to acknowledge that the source of this dynamism is concealed within this very fullness, because the source has nowhere to reside outside it; the flickering within it is the way in which it shows itself. The very ungraspability of fullness creates tension, since fullness can neither fully cast itself off nor close itself in a concept; thus, in relating to itself, it touches its own limit of ungraspability. This tension, in the absence of an outside where it could remain static, seeks an outlet, desires its own sublation (*снятие*), turning into a striving that arises within the One and is directed at the One itself. One might say that the One, as it were, strives to grasp itself in its own absence. Can one, possessing the full plenitude of meanings, ideas, and matter, fully know one’s own manifestedness without knowing one’s own absence? This gives rise to a paradox and the tension bound up with it, leading to the emergence of the impulse of desire. The emergence of this impulse of desire is thus the inevitable movement within the One whose aim is the sublation of the tension that arises, in which ungraspability calls forth grasping, beginning to take on form—not yet articulated, but already oriented toward language and logic. Thus what eludes description gives rise to the movement toward description: description will never seize fullness as a whole, but the very movement toward description, the very impulse of desire, is the way in which fullness is first articulated; it is the first moment of fullness itself, its manifestation. To show oneself is to relate to oneself, to encounter one’s own ungraspability and to undergo the tension that gives birth to the first moment in the form of the impulse of desire to be grasped, to be unfolded within oneself. The second transition is thus the passage from the recognition of the One to the recognition that the flickering within it is the inevitable movement within substance itself—the striving toward grasping that gives birth to the impulse of desire—and that this desire itself is not introduced from without but is the first moment of the original One.

### 3. The Inner Unfolding of the One

**a.** The manifestation of the first moment—that very striving to grasp which arose within the One in the second transition—does not leave the structure of the original unity unchanged. The very presence of an impulse directed from ungraspability toward grasping creates the first ontological asymmetry. The impulse of grasping is an act of self-touching in which fullness touches its own limit of ungraspability. In this act of touching there arises a gap of self-consciousness; the striving that has arisen acts as a mirror that the medium holds up to itself. This is not a rupture of integrity but its first inner articulation, in which fullness begins to “feel” itself through this impulse. Without this gap no subsequent logical articulation would be possible. In the context of our original substance this means that the ungraspable fullness for the first time acquires an inner orientation. Now, thanks to the impulse of desire, there arises within the depths of the One an implicit distinction between “source of tension” and “aim of the sublation of tension.” The first moment shows us primary distinction—not yet fixed in determinate concepts, but distinction as such, the very possibility of distinguishing. This lays the fundamental possibility for any future distinction. In order to distinguish something from something else there must exist the possibility of pointing to the directedness of the relation between them. Ungraspability that calls for grasping thereby legitimates the act of division: in order to grasp, one must single out. This means that any future logical division will not be a violence done to the original unity but the fulfilment of its inner design. This completes the formation of the basis in which the undivided ungraspable unity has acquired inner dynamism and an instrument for its own articulation, preparing the ground for the emergence of determinate forms and compositions of the cosmos. Thus the first moment that we saw in the second transition carries with it the possibility for the One to distinguish—and along with it the two moments of what is distinguished.

**b.** In the previous subsection we established the very possibility of distinction as an ontological instrument that arises in response to the manifestation of the first moment. This subsection is devoted to how this possibility is realized in determinate forms. The dynamics that were previously indescribable, having acquired the capacity for distinction, now demand a stable structure for their expression; they can no longer remain a pure impulse—they must “clothe” themselves in logic. The primary language of this formation becomes the fundamental modalities of existence: the absolute affirmation “YES” (*ДА*) and the absolute negation “NO” (*НЕТ*). Yet the transition from one to the other is not an instantaneous leap; it is a process with its own inner morphology.

At the **first stage**, “YES” is the ontological echo of the original fullness. This is not merely logical assent but the state of absolute affirmedness of being, the readiness of substance to show itself. Here there is not yet distinction, only the fact of presence, analogous to the undivided essence of the first transition. However, as was shown in the second transition, fullness contains within itself an impulse toward movement. This impulse is manifest at the **second stage**, “YES-t” (*ДАт*). This is the critical moment of the first distortion of the monolith. “YES-t” is not merely a blend of sounds; it is an ontological fissure in which the absolute affirmation “YES” still predominates, but into its structure the “virus” of negation “NO” has already been introduced. Here the dynamics of desire for the first time leave a trace in the structure of being: affirmation ceases to be self-sufficient; it becomes an affirmation that contains within itself the possibility of its own end. This is the first step toward distinction, fixed in form; being begins to “sense” its limit through the appearance of “NO” within “YES.” Thus the first two stages secure the transition from static fullness to dynamic tension, where form is still subordinate to content but already ready for transformation. The central part of the transition discloses the mechanism of distinction itself. At the **third stage**, “Rather YES than NO” (*Скорее ДА чем НЕТ*), the legitimation of duality takes place. “YES” and “NO” are singled out as full-fledged, equal moments, but they have not yet congealed in opposition—they are bound by the living tissue of doubt, “rather … than …”. This is the direct embodiment of the possibility of distinguishing; the medium learns to weigh moments within itself. The **fourth stage** is the infinite process of absolute doubt and reflection. It is the axis of symmetry of the entire transition. It is the mirror surface that the medium holds up to itself, the surface of self-touching and the gap for the self-reflection of the medium. It is not manifested by any term, because it is pure dynamics without form—the analogue of ungraspability, but now as process rather than as state. This is the point where affirmation and negation fully balance one another, vanishing in the act of pure questioning. Here thought reaches the limit of its description, touching that very indivisibility with which we began, but now at the level of logic. At this stage it is discovered that this is not the void between the poles but the very ungraspable core of the original One, which, oscillating in the absolute question, again attains its original integrity. The **fifth stage**, “YES NO” (*ДА НЕТ*), marks the hardening of the structure. As at the third stage, the moments are equal, but doubt is no longer directly manifest—it recedes into subtext, becoming the hidden mechanism of connection. Form becomes more rigid; the instrument of distinction has been used; and now we are dealing with a stable binary opposition, ready for further inclination toward negation. These three stages demonstrate how the living dynamics of doubt crystallize into logical structure. The final segment of the transition mirrorically reflects its beginning, completing the unfolding of the dynamics. The **sixth stage**, “nO-t” (*дЕТ*), is the inversion of the second stage “YES-t.” If there negation only penetrated into affirmation, here affirmation flickers within dominant negation. This is the last step on the path to absolute absence, where form still remembers being but content has already departed. The **seventh stage**, “NO,” is the terminal point—absolute negation that has completed its transition. Yet in the context of our system this is not merely an end but the completion of a cycle. Absolute negation, once attained, becomes a new form of fullness—fullness of absence.

Thus the superimposition of forms does not destroy the unity of substance but unfolds it in dynamics and logic. The seven-stage scale shows that the transition from being to nothing is not destruction but the inner movement of essence itself, which, passing through the stages of distinction and formation, remains faithful to its original impulse of grasping. Negation becomes the necessary moment for affirmation to be made conscious, closing the circle where the end is the condition of a new beginning.

**c.** The concluding phase of the third section is meant to synthesize the results obtained and to return thought to the original ontological ground, but now at a new level of awareness. The analysis shows that neither pure affirmation “YES,” nor pure negation “NO,” nor even the very process of transition between them possesses all the characteristics proper to the original One. Pure “YES” as the state of absolute affirmedness of being and pure “NO” as absolute negation and fullness of absence are not the self-sufficient fullness of the original One. If pure being were the only reality, it would be a static monolith devoid of inner life and the possibility of development—a dead congealment incapable of giving rise to distinction, which contradicts the impulse of desire embedded in fullness itself. On the other hand, if pure nothing were the only reality, there would be no medium at all in which the manifestation of the impulse of desire is possible: absence cannot strive, cannot sense itself as incompleteness, for sensation requires some presence (*наличность*). Hence both of these moments—pure being and pure nothing—are only limit-points of tension within the One, which contains both as its own immanent moments. Pure being is the moment of the One’s manifestedness to itself; pure nothing is the moment of concealment (*сокрытость*) necessary for there to be something toward which to strive. Without this duality of moments the One would remain mute and motionless; they are the necessary conditions for the drama of the cosmos, but not the whole drama. It is also easy to see that the very moment of the impulse, expressed in the process of transition, has no meaning in the absence of these two moments. Transition is a relation, and a relation requires relata. The seven-stage scale from “YES” to “NO” would hang in the void if the initial and terminal points had no ontological weight. The dynamics of desire require an object of desire and a desiring subject, even if they are fused into one. If “YES” and “NO” are removed, there remains only objectless oscillation, only the echo of ungraspable flickering. The transition is not an independent essence hovering over being and nothing; it is the living bond that stitches the poles into a single garment of reality. Thus neither pure being as absolute affirmedness, nor pure nothing as absolute negation, nor the very process of transition between them is, taken by itself, a representation of the original One. The process of transition legitimates the existence of the poles, and the poles make the process possible; but the truth lies not in them separately but in their inseparable mutual partaking (*сопричастность*) within the greater whole. On the other hand, it is not difficult to see that ontologically these three moments constitute everything into which the original One has so far unfolded—what it is in itself and in what it shows itself. Thus the original One returns to itself in the inseparable triune form of its own moments, which has acquired description and contains within itself the ungraspable and the movement toward it. This return closes the circle of transitions: we began with indivisible and ungraspable fullness, passed through the impulse of desire to the instrument of distinction and the form of formation, in order now to understand that all this complexity has not led us beyond the One but has only shown its inner structure. Triunity (*Триединство*) does not cancel the ungraspability we accepted in the first transition but includes it as the deep ground: we can describe the composition, but the substance that bears this triad remains the same all-encompassing medium. Now, however, this medium is not merely “given” to us as mystery—it is “understood” as an inseparable triune composition. The true fullness postulated in the first transition is not lost in this distinction but, on the contrary, attains its manifested structure, returning to itself in the form of Triunity. This is not the creation of a new essence but the recognition of the old one; the original One discovers that it has always contained this triadic structure as the way of its own being. This is the completion of the first major turn of the project’s philosophy: the recognition that any composition of the cosmos, any logic, and any form are not additions to the One from without but the ways in which the One lives, moves, and knows itself. The ungraspable has not vanished; it has become transparent to the gaze that holds all three moments in simultaneous tension.
